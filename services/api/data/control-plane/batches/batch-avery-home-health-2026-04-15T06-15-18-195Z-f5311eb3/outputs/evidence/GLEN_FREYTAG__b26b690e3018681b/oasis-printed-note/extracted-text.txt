Primary Reason for Home Health / Medical Necessity
The patient is considered homebound because he demonstrates significant functional limitations and medical conditions that make leaving the home difficult and unsafe without assistance. He
has an unsteady gait, poor balance, muscle weakness especially in the left upper extremity (strength 2/5), and fatigues easily. The patient also experiences dyspnea with moderate exertion and
reports a limp on the right side, further impairing safe mobility. His medical history includes recent small bowel obstruction (SBO) with hospitalization and rehabilitation, lymphedema of the lower
abdomen, chronic kidney disease stage II, obesity with BMI near 43, and left upper arm pain with limited range of motion and contractures. These factors contribute to poor endurance, requiring
considerable and taxing effort to leave the home environment. The patient's home environment presents additional safety concerns, such as lack of grab bars and handrails, rugs in the hallway,
and poor lighting, increasing fall risk. He requires assistance with activities of daily living including grooming, bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, and ambulation with assistive devices like a
cane, walker and wheelchair. While the patient is alert and oriented with intact cognitive function, he needs skilled nursing services for vital sign monitoring, medication education, disease
process teaching, and home safety evaluations due to his high risk for disease progression and hospitalization. Occupational and physical therapy are also medically necessary to improve
upper body strength, gait, balance, and overall functionality. Allowable absences from the home are limited to medically necessary appointments. Given these findings, the patient's functional
impairments and medical complexity meet Medicare criteria for homebound status and justify the need for skilled home health services to prevent injury, manage comorbidities, and facilitate
recovery.
